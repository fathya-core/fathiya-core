# Websites Are Dead. Go Here Instead.

**Published:** 2026-02-10


## I finally did it. I launched a blog. Then I realized the hard truth.


![Apple App Store, Amazon Box, LinkedIn Logo, and TikTok logo all crushing a blog with sledgehammers](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*-5yQIzx3lJzc_z2dhKHBDA.png)

Bought the domain. Picked a theme. Told myself I’d post twice a week, maybe even build an audience if the wind blew the right way.

Then I stared at my traffic numbers, if you can call “bot traffic from China” traffic, and realized something a little embarrassing:

**Nobody is coming to my little island.**  
Not when the continent is bustling a few feet away.

For years, we’ve been fed that old Field of Dreams line: _“If you build it, they will come.”_  
Turns out the internet doesn’t work like 1989 baseball movies. You can build a gorgeous blog, polish your About page, sprinkle in your best jokes, and still end up talking to yourself.

Not because your ideas are bad.  
Because people don’t wander the open web the way they used to, the mall is dead; everyone’s at Costco now.

Same thing online.

## Where the people actually are

There’s a harsh little truth I finally stopped ignoring: communities aren’t built on standalone websites anymore. They’ve moved. Migrated. Packed up and resettled in places where the attention already flows like an airport Starbucks line at 6 a.m.

If you want to be seen, you go where the crowd already is.

## App stores

App stores are the new front doors. They’re the default search engine for anything that claims to “do” something: fitness, money, recipes, sleep, productivity, whatever.  
People don’t Google for tools as much as they browse shelves.

If you’re creating anything that _does_ something:  
**ship it as an app**.  
Let Apple and Google hand you foot traffic like a mall did in the ’90s.

## Social media

Love it or hate it, and trust me, I’ve cycled through both, social platforms are the last remaining public squares.

Not because they’re healthy or noble, but because they’re easy.  
People scroll there without thinking. They bump into creators. They stumble onto ideas they didn’t search for.

You don’t have to beg them to visit your domain.  
You drop your stuff where they’re already standing.

## Marketplaces

Amazon. Etsy. Steam. The Microsoft Marketplace you’ve been poking around as a Solution Architect.  
These are _stores_ in the truest sense. And stores come with foot traffic built in.

A standalone site selling something is a lemonade stand on a quiet street.  
A listing on Amazon is a booth inside the biggest Costco on Earth. The difference in exposure is ridiculous.

## The part where I admit defeat (in a good way)

So yeah.  
I started a blog. And I’m basically giving up on it already. Not because I’m lazy, but because it feels like planting a tree in the desert and hoping people will just… sense it.

There’s no virtue in obscurity.  
No prize for taking the longest route to an audience.

If the crowd is on TikTok, or LinkedIn, or in the App Store, or browsing Amazon reviews at midnight, then that’s where I need to be. That’s where _you_ need to be if you’re building anything that deserves to be seen.

We romanticize the open web like it’s still 2008, but the reality is simpler:

**Attention has gravity. You go where it pulls.**

And weirdly, acknowledging that feels freeing.

I don’t need to shepherd people across the internet to my lonely little blog. I can meet them where they already hang out. It’s less noble, sure. But it’s practical. And honestly, I like practical.
