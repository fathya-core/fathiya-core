**1\. GBP Category Audit**

Your Google Business Profile categories control which map pack searches trigger your listing. Most business owners picked a category when they signed up and never touched it again. Meanwhile, their competitors have added secondary categories they didn’t know existed — ones that directly control which searches show their listing.

**Why it matters:**

Adding one secondary category can get you showing up for a whole new cluster of searches within the week. Fastest win in local SEO. The real power is pattern recognition — when you map categories against map pack rankings, you start seeing things. Like every business ranking for ‘emergency plumber in \[city\]’ also has ‘water damage restoration’ as a secondary category. Those patterns are invisible unless you do this analysis.

> **PROMPT:** Open Chrome and search ‘\[service\] in \[city\]’ for these 3 keywords: \[keyword1\], \[keyword2\], \[keyword3\]. For each search, open the top 3 map pack competitors and extract their primary and secondary GBP categories. Build a spreadsheet: business name, all categories, star rating, review count, ranking position. Highlight every category my competitors have that I’m missing. Rank them by how many top competitors share that category — the ones all three share are non-negotiable for me to add.

**2\. Competitor Review Velocity**

Total review count is mostly vanity. What matters is how fast they’re getting new reviews. A business with 200 reviews that went quiet 18 months ago is weaker than one with 80 reviews getting 15 a month. Google tracks this. You should too.

**Why it matters:**

The phrases customers use in 5-star reviews become your entire review request script. ‘Great furnace install in Highland Park’ is doing SEO work for your competitor whether they know it or not.

> **PROMPT** Go to these competitor GBP listings: \[URL1\], \[URL2\], \[URL3\]. For each, read their last 50 reviews and extract: total reviews, average rating, how many came in the last 30/60/90 days, the top 5 services mentioned, the top 5 locations mentioned, and the exact phrases customers use in 5-star reviews. Compare this against my reviews at \[my GBP URL\] and tell me where the gaps are.

**3\. GBP Services Section Optimization**

Google gives you a dedicated section to list services with descriptions. Most businesses leave it blank or add services with no copy at all — which is like having a product page with just a title and no content. This is prime keyword real estate and almost nobody optimizes it.

**Why it matters:**

Your services section is one of the only places on your GBP where you fully control the text. Reviews are written by customers. Q&A can come from anyone. But service descriptions? That’s your copy, your keywords, your message. If you do ‘trenchless sewer repair’ but it’s not on your GBP, you’re invisible for that search in the map pack.

> **PROMPT** Go to my GBP at \[URL\] and competitors \[URL1\], \[URL2\], \[URL3\]. Extract every service listed and whether it has a description. Cross-reference against my website at \[URL\] and flag: services I offer that aren’t in my GBP, services with no description, and services where my description is weaker than competitors. Then write optimized 40–60 word descriptions for all my services: \[service1\], \[service2\], \[service3\]. Each one should naturally include the service keyword, mention a service area, and name a specific outcome customers get.

**4\. Page 2 Goldmine (Search Console Analysis)**

Page 2 rankings are the most undervalued asset in local SEO. Someone is already searching for exactly what you do — you’re just one optimization push away from being visible. A jump from position 15 to position 5 is worth more than creating 10 new pages from scratch.

**Why it matters:**

Most businesses are sitting on page 2 rankings that are one title tag change away from page 1. This audit finds them all and gives you exact copy to fix — no guesswork, no wasted effort.

> **PROMPT** Log into Google Search Console for \[yourdomain.com\]. Pull the last 90 days of data. Find every keyword where I rank between position 11 and 20 with at least 100 monthly impressions. For each: check if the keyword is in the title tag, H1, and first 100 words. Note word count and internal links pointing to that page. Build a 30-day sprint: week 1 = title and H1 fixes, week 2 = content additions for thin pages, week 3 = internal linking fixes, week 4 = meta description rewrites. Write the exact copy for every single change — not instructions, the actual words to use.

**5\. Service + City Page Builder**

Google ranks pages, not websites. If you don’t have a page specifically about \[service\] in \[city\], you won’t rank for that search. Most local businesses have one generic service page and wonder why they don’t show up in cities 30 minutes away.

**Why it matters:**

This prompt builds your entire location page stack in one session. The work that would take a team of writers weeks gets done before lunch.

> **PROMPT** My primary service is \[service\]. My target cities are \[city1\], \[city2\], \[city3\], \[city4\], \[city5\]. First check my site at \[URL\] and tell me which city + service pages already exist. Then for every missing combination, write a fully optimized page: SEO title under 60 chars, meta description under 155 chars, H1 with service and city, 100-word opening paragraph, 150-word local section mentioning area landmarks or neighborhoods, 200-word service detail section, FAQ with 3 city-specific questions, and a CTA with phone number. Also give me the URL slug and 3 internal link opportunities per page.

**6\. Review Sentiment Analysis**

This is the prompt most agencies skip entirely. It takes your competitors’ reviews and reverse-engineers the exact emotional language customers use — then builds that language into your website copy, GBP content, and review request scripts.

**Why it matters:**

Most businesses write their own website copy about themselves. The businesses that dominate write their copy in the language their customers actually use. Review sentiment analysis finds that language — the exact words that make a stranger trust you enough to call.

> **PROMPT** Go to these competitor GBP listings: \[URL1\], \[URL2\], \[URL3\]. Read the last 100 reviews for each. Extract: the top 20 emotional words used most, the top 10 specific outcomes mentioned, the top 5 fears or frustrations mentioned before the service, and the exact phrases that appear in 5-star reviews but not in 3-star ones. Do the same for my reviews at \[my GBP URL\]. Then use all of this to rewrite: my GBP description, my homepage headline, my review request script, and 3 social proof statements for my website.

**7\. Citation Audit**

Inconsistent NAP data — your name, address, and phone listed differently across directories — is one of the most common local SEO killers. Google cross-references your business information across hundreds of sources. If your phone number is different on Yelp than on Google, it creates a trust signal conflict that suppresses your rankings. Most businesses have no idea how many bad citations are working against them.

**Why it matters:**

Fixing citations is one of the few SEO tasks where you can see real ranking improvements within 30 days of making the changes.

> **PROMPT** My exact business info: Name: \[exact name\], Address: \[exact address\], Phone: \[phone\], Website: \[URL\]. Search for my listing across: Google Business Profile, Yelp, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Facebook, BBB, Angi, Thumbtack, Yellow Pages, Foursquare, and any industry-specific directories for \[your industry\]. For each, record the name, address, phone, and URL exactly as listed. Flag every inconsistency and give me step-by-step instructions for correcting each one. Also list high-value directories where I have no listing.

**8\. Competitor Backlink Pattern**

You don’t need hundreds of backlinks. You need the right ones — the same ones your competitors already earned. This prompt finds exactly where they’re getting their authority from and tells you exactly what to say when you reach out.

**Why it matters:**

Two to four real contextual links per month compounds faster than 20 random directory submissions. This removes all the guesswork — you know who to target, how they earned the link, and what to say.

> **PROMPT** In Ahrefs, pull the backlink profile for \[competitor1.com\], \[competitor2.com\], \[competitor3.com\] filtered to: dofollow only, DR 20+, referring domain traffic 100+/month, no sitewide links. Find domains that link to all 3 competitors but not to me — those are highest priority. For each one, tell me the domain type, how the competitor likely earned the link, my realistic chance of getting a similar one, and write the full outreach email I can send today. Build a 90-day link plan: month 1 = easy wins, month 2 = medium effort, month 3 = authority plays.

**9\. Entity Optimization**

Most local SEOs don’t know this lever exists. Google doesn’t just rank websites — it ranks entities. Your business needs to exist as a verified entity in Google’s knowledge graph to unlock the highest level of local trust signals. Businesses with strong entity signals rank higher, show up more in AI Overviews, and are more resilient to algorithm updates.

**Why it matters:**

This is where local SEO is going. Building it now, while most competitors aren’t doing it, is the biggest compounding advantage available.

> **PROMPT** My business details: Name: \[name\], Address: \[address\], Phone: \[phone\], Website: \[URL\], GBP: \[URL\], Founded: \[year\], Owner: \[name\]. Search ‘\[business name\] \[city\]’ and ‘\[owner name\] \[business name\]’ and tell me what appears — does a knowledge panel show? Check wikidata.org for my business name. Run my website through Google’s Rich Results Test and tell me what schema is implemented and what’s missing. Then write the complete LocalBusiness JSON-LD schema code I need to add to my homepage, list authoritative sites where I need to build a presence, and give me instructions for triggering a knowledge panel.

**10\. Monthly SEO Report That Actually Matters**

Most business owners track the wrong things. Total traffic and domain rating don’t tell you if SEO is making you money. Calls and revenue from organic are the only numbers that connect directly to your bank account. This report keeps you focused on those — and nothing else.

**Why it matters:**

If you can’t measure it you can’t manage it. But measuring the wrong things is worse than measuring nothing. This report is built to be read in 5 minutes and acted on the same day.

> **PROMPT** Pull data from Google Search Console, Google Business Profile Insights, and Google Analytics 4 for \[yourdomain.com\] — last 30 days vs previous 30 days. From GSC: total clicks, impressions, CTR, average position, top 10 keywords by clicks, top 10 that improved, top 10 that dropped. From GBP: profile views, branded vs discovery searches, calls, direction requests, website clicks, photo views, new reviews. From GA4: organic sessions, conversion rate, top landing pages. Build a one-page report with 3 wins, 3 problems, 1 priority action for next month, and whether calls from GBP went up or down. Readable in 5 minutes.

**How to run these in order**

Don’t try to run all 10 at once. Here’s the sequence that works:

*   Week 1: Prompts 1 and 7 (GBP categories + citation audit). Fastest fixes with the most immediate ranking impact.
*   Week 2: Prompts 2 and 6 (review velocity + sentiment analysis). Now you have a review strategy and messaging that actually resonates.
*   Week 3: Prompts 3 and 5 (GBP services + city pages). Your GBP and website start working together.
*   Week 4: Prompts 4 and 8 (page 2 wins + backlinks). The authority and quick wins phase.
*   Week 5–6: Prompts 9 and 10 (entity + monthly tracking). Your long-term competitive moat starts forming.

90 days of consistent execution on this system and you’ll outrank businesses that have been established for years. Not because the prompts are magic — because most of your competitors aren’t doing any of it.
